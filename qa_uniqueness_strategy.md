## Lever 1 — Be the only one who sat with a QA before the interview

Shashid literally asked Barat: *"Did you try to sit with a QA and understand what exactly they do before coming for this interview?"*

Barat said no. Everyone said no.

This one action — spending 20–30 minutes with a QA on your floor before your interview — will make you the only candidate who can say yes.

### What to do

Find a QA on your floor or in your network. Sit with them for 20–30 minutes. Watch them do an audit. Ask them what their hardest part of the day is. Ask them how they handle a difficult reviewer. You do not need to become an expert — you just need to have done it.

### What to say in the interview

> *"Before this interview, I sat with one of the QAs on our floor for about 30 minutes to observe their audit process and understand the day-to-day realities of the role. I wanted to make sure I was not just preparing theory — I wanted to see what the job actually looks like in practice."*

That one sentence will make Shashid stop and pay attention. No one else did this. No one even thought of it. Barat said no and Shashid's tone shifted immediately.

## Lever 2 — Speak in structure, not in rambling

Every single candidate gave paragraphs of words that circled the answer when asked about QA roles. Shashid had to keep asking "yes, and what else?" every time.

### What every candidate sounded like

*"QA is about maintaining quality and helping reviewers and checking audits and giving feedback and also doing huddles and refreshers and supporting..."*

### What you will sound like

*"A QA has five core areas of responsibility. First — audits, specifically Koala, safety net, stratified, random, and tool tests. Second — quality reports prepared weekly. Third — daily SOD huddles and MOM emails for team communication. Fourth — one-on-one feedback using the sandwich method. Fifth — OJT batch support including random audits and weekly quality reports for new joiners. Beyond these, a QA also handles client escalations through RCA and gives refresher sessions when policy updates come in."*

The difference is not the information — it is the structure. Numbered answers sound confident. They sound prepared. They make Shashid feel like he is talking to someone who has thought deeply about the role.

**Rule for every answer:** pause one second, say "there are [N] things I want to cover," then go through them with "first... second... third." Every time.

## Lever 3 — Volunteer information before being asked

Every candidate waited to be asked. Shashid had to drag information out of them. You are going to do the opposite — proactively add things he did not ask for. This signals that you genuinely know the subject, not just the answers to his questions.

### Moments where you can volunteer

**When talking about QA roles** — after the 5 areas, add:
> *"I also want to mention Meta QA. When a QA cannot resolve a doubt independently, they escalate to Meta QA, who connects with the client or other vendors if needed and cascades the answer back down to all QAs."*

**When talking about feedback** — after the sandwich method, add:
> *"If a reviewer continues making errors even after structured feedback and monitoring, the next step under policy is PCCP — Performance Counseling and Coaching Program — where they are monitored for 4 weeks under a formal plan."*

**When talking about quality tools** — do not wait to be asked:
> *"I also prepared the 7 quality tools in case that is helpful — fishbone, check sheet, control chart, histogram, Pareto chart, scatter diagram, and stratification."*

**When talking about precision and recall** — after the formula, add:
> *"I also want to clarify that in our context, a false negative is often more dangerous than a false positive — a missed bad account causes more harm than wrongly suspending an ok account, which is why recall is closely monitored."*

### Important — do not overdo it

Volunteer one or two extra things, not ten. Each extra thing should be said in 1–2 sentences, cleanly, and then stop. The goal is to create moments of "oh, this person knows more than I asked." Not to give a lecture.

## Lever 4 — Connect your past work to QA thinking

Every candidate talked about their past as a separate thing. "I worked in risk enforcement. I worked in NHR. I worked in ERT." None of them said: "Here is how my past work gives me an advantage as a QA."

You are going to make that connection explicitly.

### What to say

> *"My time as a rater has given me a direct understanding of what reviewers struggle with — where policies are confusing, where errors happen, what kind of feedback actually helps versus what feels like a scolding. I know what it feels like to be on the reviewer side, which means as a QA I can give feedback that is accurate, empathetic, and actually useful — not just technically correct."*

This is powerful because it is true — and no one else said it. Being a rater before becoming a QA is not a weakness. It is a perspective advantage. You know the floor. You know what it is like to be audited. That makes you a better QA than someone who came in fresh.

## Lever 5 — Show self-awareness about growth

Managers do not just want skilled people — they want people who know their own gaps and are actively working on them. Saiprasanna said "I'll prepare better next time" after the interview ended — too late, wrong moment. You are going to do this strategically inside the interview.

### When and how to use this

If you blank on something, or if asked about weaknesses:

> *"I want to be honest — I have not yet had hands-on experience with all 7 quality tools in practice. I know them conceptually and I prepared them for this interview, but I understand there is a difference between knowing something and applying it. My plan is to shadow a QA for the first few weeks and specifically look for how these tools get used in real calibration sessions — so that by the time I am running my own audits, I am using them properly and not just from memory."*

This kind of answer shows maturity. It does not hide a gap — it names it and immediately shows a plan. Anyone can pretend to know everything. Very few people say "here is what I do not yet know, and here is how I will fix that."

## Lever 6 — Ask the best closing question

Most candidates said no when asked "do you have any questions for me?" You are going to ask a question that is about the role and the team — which signals that you are already thinking like a QA, not like a candidate hoping to get selected.

### Primary question — ask this one

> *"What does the first 30 days look like for a new QA on your team? Is there a calibration period or do new QAs start auditing independently right away? I want to understand how to ramp up the right way."*

### Backup question

> *"What is the most common mistake you see new QAs make in their first few weeks — the thing that takes the longest to correct? I want to know what to actively watch out for."*

Both of these questions show you are thinking beyond getting selected. You are thinking about how to perform. That is a QA mindset — proactive, structured, forward-looking.

## Lever 7 — Your communication style during the interview

Communication style is invisible — candidates never think about it consciously. But Shashid responds to it very clearly. Look at how often he interrupted candidates who rambled, moved on when answers repeated, and stayed engaged when answers were clean and structured.

### What loses his attention

- Starting answers with "uh" and "so basically"
- Repeating the question back to him before answering
- Saying the same thing twice in different words
- Trailing off without a clear ending
- Answering a different question than what he asked

### What keeps his attention

- Starting with the direct answer immediately
- Numbered structure: "First... second... third..."
- Stopping when the answer is complete — not over-explaining
- Pausing briefly before answering (shows thinking, not hesitation)
- Using specific workflow words (Koala, SOD, MOM, PCCP, stratified)
- Saying "in our workflow context" — shows you connect theory to practice

### The pause-and-structure technique

When asked a complex question, do not rush. Take one second. Then say: *"There are three things I want to cover here — first..."*

This tells him you are going to be organised. It also buys you 2–3 seconds to think. Every candidate rushed and rambled. A brief pause before a structured answer is a sign of confidence, not hesitation.

## Lever 8 — The mindset shift that changes everything

Most candidates walked in thinking: *"I need to show him I know the answers."*

The right mindset is: *"I need to show him I can do the job."*

There is a difference. Knowing the precision formula is an answer. Saying "as a QA I would use precision to identify which reviewers are over-flagging and target my refresher sessions at those specific cases" — that is showing you can do the job.

### How to apply this in every answer

After giving any factual answer, add one sentence about what you would do with that knowledge as a QA.

**After precision formula:**
> *"So if precision drops, I know I need to look at false positives — reviewers who are over-flagging — and target my feedback sessions at those specific error types."*

**After RCA explanation:**
> *"I would prepare the RCA document within 24 hours of a client escalation, identify whether it is a reviewer miss or a system issue, and give feedback that same week so the reviewer corrects before their next audit cycle."*

**After sandwich method:**
> *"The reason I use the sandwich method is not just to soften the message — it is because reviewers who feel respected during feedback are more likely to actually change behaviour. The goal is always to improve their accuracy, not to make them defensive."*

That extra sentence — "here is what I would do with this as a QA" — is what separates a candidate who memorised answers from a candidate who is ready to actually do the job.
